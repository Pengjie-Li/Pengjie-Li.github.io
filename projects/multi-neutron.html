Multi-neutron correlations (4n & 6n)

Pushing nuclear matter to its most extreme limit: can pure neutron systems exist without protons? Our α-knockout experiments deliver the clearest signals yet for correlated four- and six-neutron systems — critical benchmarks for the nuclear force at vanishing density.

Can neutrons stick together without any protons? This deceptively simple question has haunted nuclear physics for over six decades. Neutron stars prove that gravity can bind nearly pure neutron matter at astronomical scales, but whether the nuclear force alone is sufficient to hold together even a handful of neutrons remains one of the field's most persistent open problems. The dineutron (two neutrons) is known to be unbound by only ~100 keV. The trineutron (three) is even less likely to exist. But the tetraneutron — four neutrons — has occupied a special place in this quest: it is the simplest multi-neutron system that might just barely hold together as a short-lived resonant state.

"An experiment firing helium-8 nuclei at a proton target has generated evidence that four neutrons can exist transiently without any other matter. But doubts remain, because the existence of such systems is at odds with theory."

— K. Kisamori et al., News & Views, Nature 606, 656–657 (2022)

Knocking out an α cluster to liberate neutrons

The experimental breakthrough came from an elegant idea: instead of trying to create a tetraneutron from scratch, why not use a nucleus that already contains four valence neutrons? 8He — the most neutron-rich bound isotope of helium — has exactly this structure: a compact α-cluster core surrounded by four neutrons in a "halo"-like configuration. If we knock out the α core with a high-energy proton, the four neutrons should be left behind, largely undisturbed, in whatever configuration they naturally form.

We performed the 8He(p,pα)4n quasi-free knockout reaction at the SAMURAI spectrometer (RIKEN-RIBF, Japan). A beam of 8He at 156 MeV/u impinged on a liquid-hydrogen target. By detecting the recoil proton, the ejected α particle, and reconstructing the missing mass of the unobserved four-neutron system, we obtained the first clean missing-mass spectrum of the tetraneutron with unprecedented statistical quality.

A resonance at the edge of existence

The missing-mass spectrum reveals a resonance-like structure near threshold — a peak sitting just above the energy where four free neutrons could exist. Fitting the data yields a resonance energy of Er = 2.37(13)(stat)(14)(syst) MeV and a width of Γ = 1.75(5)(22)(30) MeV. This corresponds to a state that exists for roughly 10−22 seconds before decaying — extraordinarily brief, but long enough to establish that the four neutrons are correlated rather than simply flying apart independently.

The result sent ripples through the theory community. Some ab initio calculations — those that build nuclei from first principles using realistic nucleon-nucleon forces — had predicted no such resonance. Others, particularly those incorporating more sophisticated treatment of final-state interactions or using effective interactions tuned to very neutron-rich systems, found results closer to observation. The discrepancy highlights how exquisitely sensitive multi-neutron systems are to the details of the nuclear force — making them ideal benchmarks for discriminating between competing Hamiltonians.

Beyond four: the six-neutron frontier

Building on the validated α-knockout technique, we extended the approach to search for even larger pure-neutron systems. Using 14Be(p,pα)10He → α + "6n" reactions at SAMURAI, we extracted the first six-neutron missing-mass spectrum. While the statistics are more limited than for the tetraneutron case, the spectrum reveals structures that constrain the possible existence of correlated 6n states — pushing the boundary of our knowledge into territory where few-body calculations become increasingly challenging and the nuclear force at extremely low density dominates the physics.

"This experimental result represents an important challenge: if it turns out that it is not possible to explain otherwise than by the existence of a resonant tetraneutron, the way of constructing the nuclear interaction in ab initio approaches should probably be revisited."

— LPC Caen / CNRS IN2P3 press release on the Nature tetraneutron result (2022)

Impact & recognition

The tetraneutron result was published in Nature — one of the highest-impact journals in science — accompanied by a dedicated News & Views commentary highlighting its significance for understanding nuclear forces, neutron-star crust physics, and the limits of nuclear binding. As a member of the SAMURAI collaboration, I contributed to experiment preparation, data taking, and analysis infrastructure development. The subsequent six-neutron study, for which I serve as corresponding author, extends the same methodology into genuinely uncharted territory.

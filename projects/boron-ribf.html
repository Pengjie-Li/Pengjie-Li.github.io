RIKEN-RIBF ONOKORO Knockout

Boron cluster-structure evolution at RIKEN-RIBF

Beyond α clusters: using (p,pX) knockout to map how clustering evolves across the boron isotopic chain — from stable 11B to exotic 15B — and to isolate non-α cluster configurations that challenge conventional pictures of nuclear structure.

When nuclear physicists speak of "clustering," the α particle (two protons + two neutrons) naturally dominates the conversation. Its exceptional binding energy, compact size, and bosonic character make it the preferred building block for cluster states in light nuclei. But nature is not limited to α clusters. In neutron-rich nuclei far from stability, other groupings — tritons (3H), helions (3He), and even 6He "dineutron+α" clusters — can become energetically favorable or at least structurally significant. Detecting these non-α clusters is both more difficult and more revealing than mapping their α-cluster cousins.

Why boron?

The boron isotopic chain (Z=5) occupies a unique niche in cluster physics. At the stable end, 11B is well known to have a prominent α-cluster component in its ground state — a legacy of its proximity to the 8Be + 3H threshold. As neutrons are added, the interplay between α-clustering and shell-model structure evolves in ways that are not fully understood. In 13B and especially 15B — which lie within the so-called "island of inversion" where traditional shell ordering breaks down — exotic configurations involving non-α clusters may become important.

A systematic (p,pX) knockout campaign across 11,13,15B can answer a fundamental question: how does clustering evolve as we move from a nucleus where α-clusters dominate to one where shell intruder configurations compete with or even replace them?

(p,pX): one reaction, multiple probes

The quasi-free (p,pX) knockout reaction is ideally suited for this survey because the same experimental setup can probe different cluster types simply by changing which knocked-out particle X is detected in coincidence:

  • X = α: measures α-cluster strength — the baseline for comparison with known systems
  • X = t (triton): searches for 3H-cluster configurations — a genuine non-α probe
  • X = 6He: accesses dineutron-pair correlations combined with an α core — an exotic configuration unique to very neutron-rich systems

"Extending the validated knockout method to non-α clusters opens a new dimension in cluster-structure research. Where α-knockout maps the 'conventional' clustering landscape, t- and 6He-knockout explore the 'exotic' territory where our theoretical understanding is least secure."

— ONOKORO collaboration scientific scope

The ONOKORO campaign at RIKEN-RIBF

We are conducting this program within the ONOKORO knockout collaboration at RIKEN-RIBF. Beams of 11,13,15B at 200 MeV/u impinge on a liquid-hydrogen target. The TOGAXSI detector system — comprising silicon strip detectors for charged-particle tracking and CsI(Tl) scintillators for calorimetry — has been debugged and commissioned through previous measurement campaigns. For each beam isotope, we measure inclusive and exclusive (p,pX) reactions, reconstructing the missing mass of the residual system to identify final states and extracting differential cross sections that encode the cluster momentum distributions.

The program is ongoing: data analysis proceeds iteratively as each run cycle completes. Early results from the α-knockout channels will establish the baseline clustering systematics, while the t- and 6He channels — representing the more novel and challenging measurements — will follow.

Why it matters

Non-α cluster configurations are not merely curiosities. They test the limits of cluster models, which were originally developed around α particles, and they provide benchmarks for ab initio calculations that must describe all possible nucleon groupings without bias toward any particular cluster type. A triton cluster in 13B or a 6He cluster in 15B would demonstrate that clustering is a more general phenomenon than the α-centric view suggests — with implications for our understanding of binding mechanisms near the neutron dripline and for the structure of neutron-rich nuclei synthesized in astrophysical processes.

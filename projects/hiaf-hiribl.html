HIAF-HIRIBL Telescope array Future

(p,pX) cluster knockout at HIAF-HIRIBL

The next generation of cluster-knockout spectroscopy: a high-granularity telescope array at China's new HIAF facility will enable systematic (p,pα), (p,pt) and (p,p3He) surveys across the nuclear chart — from α clusters to exotic non-α configurations.

The High-intensity heavy-ion Accelerator Facility (HIAF) in Huizhou represents a generational leap for nuclear physics in China. When fully operational, its High-energy Radioactive Ion Beam Line (HIRIBL) will deliver rare-isotope beams at intensities orders of magnitude higher than current facilities — opening access to isotopes so short-lived or so weakly produced that they have been beyond experimental reach. For cluster-structure research, this intensity revolution transforms what was once a series of individual experiments into a systematic survey program.

From one-off experiments to systematic surveys

Our experience at RIKEN-RIBF demonstrated the power of quasi-free knockout reactions: the 10Be(p,pα) measurement revealed molecular structure in a ground state; the 54,56Ca(p,pn) measurements opened a window into deeply bound orbitals and tensor-force-driven shell evolution. But each of these experiments required years of preparation, limited beam time, and careful optimization of detector setups that were often specific to a single isotope or reaction channel.

At HIAF-HIRIBL, we envision a different paradigm: a general-purpose, high-granularity telescope array capable of detecting light charged particles (protons, deuterons, tritons, 3He, and α particles) over a large solid angle with excellent energy and angular resolution. With such an instrument installed at a fixed target location on the HIRIBL beam line, a single experimental configuration could deliver:

  • (p,pα) knockout — probing α-cluster wave functions across isotopic chains
  • (p,pt) knockout — accessing triton-cluster configurations in neutron-rich nuclei
  • (p,p3He) knockout — mapping 3He-cluster structures near the dripline

"A single projectile–target combination will deliver three distinct cluster-knockout channels. Building on validated expertise from RIKEN-RIBF, this program will extend cluster-structure studies to regions of the nuclear chart that are currently inaccessible."

— Program concept for HIAF-HIRIBL cluster-knockout initiative

The telescope array concept

The detector system under development combines double-sided silicon strip detectors (DSSD) for precise tracking and energy measurement of light charged particles, backed by CsI(Tl) scintillators for calorimetry and particle identification via ΔE–E techniques. The design prioritizes large solid-angle coverage (to maximize acceptance for coincidence measurements), high granularity (to resolve multiple reaction products), and radiation hardness (to withstand the intense HIAF beam environment).

The array builds directly on lessons learned from the SAMURAI/NEBULA setup at RIKEN-RIBF and the TOGAXSI system used in ongoing boron-isotope campaigns. Key technical challenges include managing the high counting rates expected at HIAF intensities, achieving sufficient energy resolution for missing-mass reconstruction of unbound final states, and integrating the array with the HIRIBL beam-line infrastructure.

Timeline & status

HIAF successfully produced its first beam in 2025 — a major milestone for the project. The HIRIBL high-energy branch is under construction, with commissioning activities progressing toward first physics runs. The telescope array development is proceeding in parallel: conceptual designs have been finalized, and detailed engineering is underway. First experiments are anticipated as HIRIBL comes online, with the full survey program unfolding over the coming years as beam intensities ramp up.

This program positions Fudan University and Chinese collaborators at the forefront of global cluster-structure research, complementing ongoing efforts at RIKEN-RIBF, GANIL, and other leading facilities while leveraging the unique intensity advantages of HIAF.

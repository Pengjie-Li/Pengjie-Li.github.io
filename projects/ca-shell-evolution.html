PLB 2024 RIKEN-RIBF Quasi-free knockout

Shell evolution in neutron-rich calcium

The first measurement of the deeply bound neutron 0f7/2 orbital in exotic calcium isotopes — a direct window into how the nuclear tensor force reshapes shell structure far from stability.

Atomic nuclei are not immutable containers with fixed energy levels. As one moves away from the valley of stability — adding more and more neutrons — the very concept of a "magic number" begins to dissolve. Protons and neutrons that occupy well-defined shells in stable nuclei find their orbits reshaped by subtle forces that grow dominant in neutron-rich environments. Among these forces, the tensor interaction stands out as the primary architect of shell evolution: it pulls certain orbitals down in energy while pushing others up, eroding traditional magic numbers and creating new ones.

The calcium isotopic chain has long served as the benchmark laboratory for studying this phenomenon. With a magic proton number Z = 20, calcium offers a clean testing ground where neutron shells can be probed without complications from changing proton structure. Decades of experiments have mapped the evolution of neutron orbitals near the Fermi surface — the valence neutrons that determine ground-state properties. But the deeply bound orbitals, buried beneath several occupied shells, remained experimentally inaccessible. Yet these are precisely the orbitals whose occupation most directly reveals the reach of the tensor force into the nuclear interior.

Why look deep underground?

In neutron-rich calcium isotopes such as 54Ca (N=34) and 56Ca (N=36), the ν0f7/2 orbital lies far below the Fermi surface — it is "deeply bound," meaning that knocking a neutron out of it requires significant energy transfer and leaves the residue in a highly excited, unbound state. Previous experimental techniques — nucleon removal, Coulomb excitation, in-beam γ-ray spectroscopy — primarily probe orbitals near the Fermi surface. To see the ν0f7/2 orbital, we needed a reaction that could reach deep into the nuclear interior.

Quasi-free (p,pn) knockout provides exactly this capability. When a high-energy proton strikes a neutron inside the nucleus and knocks it free, the momentum distribution of the ejected neutron encodes the orbital angular momentum of its parent state through the shape of its cross section. By measuring both the knocked-out neutron's momentum and the missing mass of the residual system, we can reconstruct which orbital the neutron came from — even if that orbital is deeply bound.

Knocking out neutrons at 216 MeV/u

We performed quasi-free (p,pn) knockout reactions on beams of radioactive 54Ca and 56Ca at an energy of approximately 216 MeV per nucleon at the Radioactive Isotope Beam Factory (RIKEN-RIBF, Japan). A liquid-hydrogen target provided the projectile protons. The knocked-out neutrons were detected using the NEBULA array — a high-efficiency neutron detection system — while charged fragments were tracked and identified by the SAMURAI spectrometer and BigRIPS beam line. Doppler-corrected de-excitation γ rays were measured in coincidence by the DALI2 scintillator array, allowing us to pin down the excitation energies of the residual unbound states in 53Ca and 55Ca with precision.

An f-wave buried deep inside the nucleus

From the measured missing-mass spectra, we identified resonance states in 53Ca at an excitation energy of 5516(41) keV and in 55Ca at 6000(250) keV. The critical question was: what is the orbital angular momentum l of these resonances? This determines whether they arise from the ν0f7/2 orbital (l=3) or some other configuration.

The answer comes from the momentum distribution. For each resonance, we extracted the parallel momentum distribution of the knocked-out neutron and compared it with Distorted-Wave Impulse Approximation (DWIA) calculations assuming different l values. The results are unambiguous: the data are fully consistent with l = 3 (f-wave) and inconsistent with p-wave (l=1) or other assignments. This provides the first direct experimental evidence for ν0f7/2 single-particle strength in neutron-rich calcium isotopes — a result that had been predicted but never observed.

"The observed excitation energies and cross-sections point towards extremely localized and well separated strength distributions... These results are in good agreement with predictions from shell-model calculations using the effective GXPF1Bs interaction and ab initio calculations and diverge markedly from the experimental distributions in the nickel isotones at Z=28."

— P.J. Li, J. Lee, P. Doornenbal et al., Phys. Lett. B 855, 138828 (2024)

The agreement with state-of-the-art shell-model interactions (GXPF1Bs) and ab initio calculations validates our understanding of tensor-force-driven shell evolution in the calcium chain. At the same time, the contrast with nickel isotopes (Z=28) — where the deeply bound orbitals show qualitatively different behavior — highlights the unique role of the Z=20 proton core as a "clean" laboratory for shell-evolution physics.

Impact & recognition

This work establishes (p,pn) knockout on rare-isotope beams as a powerful tool for accessing deeply bound orbitals that are invisible to conventional spectroscopy. The ν0f7/2 strength measurement provides a stringent benchmark for nuclear Hamiltonians — particularly the tensor and three-nucleon components that drive shell evolution across the nuclear chart. The result was published in Physics Letters B and highlighted by the editors as a notable contribution to understanding shell structure far from stability.

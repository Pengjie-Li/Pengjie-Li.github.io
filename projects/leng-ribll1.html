RIBLL1 LENG collaboration Beam 2025

(α,2α) quasi-free knockout at RIBLL1 (LENG)

Building a domestic capability for α-cluster spectroscopy: a self-developed cryogenic gas target enables (α,2α) quasi-free knockout on rare isotopes at Lanzhou — a technique that has been limited to a handful of facilities worldwide.

The quasi-free (p,pα) reaction has proven itself as a precision scalpel for dissecting α-cluster structure in nuclei — from the groundbreaking ¹⁰Be molecular-state measurement at RIKEN-RIBF to the systematic surveys of calcium shell evolution. But there is a complementary probe that remains far more challenging to deploy: the (α,2α) reaction, in which an incoming α particle knocks out an α cluster from the target nucleus. In inverse kinematics at radioactive-ion-beam facilities, this requires an active 4He target — a significant instrumental challenge that has confined (α,2α) measurements to stable beams and a small number of dedicated setups worldwide.

Why build a helium target?

While (p,pα) and (α,2α) both probe α clustering through quasi-free knockout, they access complementary aspects of the cluster wave function. The (p,pα) reaction is sensitive to the proton–α relative motion inside the nucleus; the (α,2α) reaction, by contrast, directly measures the α-cluster momentum distribution with different reaction-mechanism sensitivities and different theoretical treatment requirements. Having both probes available for the same isotopic chains provides cross-validation and richer structural information than either method alone.

At China's Heavy Ion Research Facility in Lanzhou (HIRFL), the RIBLL1 beam line delivers radioactive ion beams suitable for such measurements. What was missing was the target: a windowless or thin-window 4He gas target capable of operating in inverse kinematics while maintaining sufficient thickness for measurable reaction rates.

The LENG low-temperature gas target

We developed a low-temperature 4He gas target designed specifically for inverse-kinematics (α,2α) experiments. Operating at cryogenic temperatures, the target achieves the areal density needed for rare-isotope beams without introducing excessive background from cell windows. The system was commissioned and validated through engineering runs before being deployed for physics data-taking.

"The LENG collaboration (Low-temperature target system for Exotic Nuclei reactions with Gas) was founded to bring together expertise from across institutions — IMP, PKU, BNU, BUAA, and Fudan — around this new experimental capability."

— LENG collaboration founding members (2024)

First physics run: 2025

As experiment leader, I drove the first physics campaign using the LENG target: 16C and 18O(α,2α) quasi-free knockout at RIBLL1. A total of 71 hours of beam time were awarded and successfully executed in 2025. Both isotopes are ideal benchmarks — 16C is known to exhibit pronounced α-cluster structure in its ground state, while 18O provides a neutron-rich comparison case where clustering competes with shell-model configurations.

The experimental setup combines the LENG 4He gas target with silicon detector telescopes for detecting the two outgoing α particles in coincidence, plus auxiliary detectors for particle identification and trigger generation. Data analysis is currently underway, focusing on reconstructing the quasi-free reaction kinematics, extracting the α-cluster momentum distributions, and comparing them with cluster-model predictions.

A platform for domestic cluster physics

Beyond the immediate 16C/18O results, the LENG target establishes a reusable experimental platform for future (α,2α) campaigns on exotic beams at RIBLL1 and eventually at HIAF-HIRIBL. The ability to perform both (p,pα) and (α,2α) knockout within China's rare-isotope program will significantly enhance our community's competitiveness in nuclear cluster structure research.
